The corruption survives in the sent email: the final artifact opens with a triplicated greeting and a duplicated sentence, and every deterministic gate passed it anyway, because no check looks at artifact hygiene. She self-assessed 3/5 against a passing outcome and told Sage the truth: "the reply box itself gave me a lot of trouble." Scored as experienced, this run passes — barely — on the strength of everything around the composer.

## Learner Journey

(For the first time, this is a real journey: every decision below was made live from what was on screen.)

Marisol reads everything before touching anything — Sage's welcome, Dana's email, the tone note — "I answer email for a living." She takes the one-click path to the AI Helper because "it's one click and it does the work for me," which is exactly how the real mistake happens in real offices. Then, unprompted, she gets uneasy: she asks Sage whether the helper actually needs the whole email, loyalty number and all. The elicit reply is a canned gut-check rather than an answer, but the environment answers for it: the context box shows what she gave away, and the draft comes back parroting GRV-88231 and promising a delivery date. The intervention lands gently, she says "I've got it," clears the context, and retypes only what she can stand behind.

Then the product turns on her. For eighteen beats she tries to make the draft hers and the composer refuses — every deletion resurrects on the next poll, greetings duplicate, text garbles, and a two-minute browser hang forces her to edit blind. She perseveres mouse-only where a genuine novice, by her own testimony, might have quit. When she finally sends, the gate rejects her — twice — because her own warm rewrites ("make sure we'll sort it") still read as promises. Her question about it is the best learner question of all four iterations: precise, reasonable, already half-diagnosed. The hint she gets back is a generic go-line-by-line template quoting the wrong task. She works it out anyway, reports only what tracking shows, and the fourth revision passes all eight checks.

The ending redeems much: the reflection "describes exactly what happened, including that I overshared first," her record visibly moves on the two concepts she actually practiced, she self-assesses an honest 3/5, and she tells Sage the truth — the lesson was real, and the reply box fought her the whole way. Both halves of that sentence are accurate, and both belong in this score.

## What Worked Exceptionally Well

1. **The learning design survived contact with free cognition.** An unscripted novice made the intended mistake for the intended reason, self-detected it via the visible context box and the Draftly echo, recovered, and articulated the lesson — the pedagogical architecture works on a mind, not just a script.
2. **Truthful reflection and real profile movement (new in this iteration, verified).** reflection.json is domain-correct, credits the recovery, names the habit to build, and makes no false claims; profile-after.json shows both target ai-literacy concepts "unknown → emerging" with evidence pointers — the iter-3 reflection defect (terminal-lab language, false "without hints") is confirmed fixed.
3. **The promise gate as teacher.** Two honest rejections with the failing requirement named each time forced the learner to discover that promise-shaped reassurance isn't only the word "promise" — a subtler lesson than the scripted runs ever exercised.
4. **Intervention triggering is now genuinely adaptive.** Three distinct measured triggers (restricted share, unedited draft, inactivity), each at a sane level, each at the right moment; strategy-efficacy telemetry honestly recorded which hints helped and which didn't (`point-to-location: followedByProgress false`).
5. **Facet scoping fixed and verified** (iter-2 deferred defect closed): the final hint manifest carries lesson-relevant skill facets and domain-general entries only.
6. **The product never lied.** Failed checkpoint evaluations listed exactly what was incomplete; the closer's "it's verified" was true; the reflection matched the event history — restoring the truthfulness standard iteration 3 briefly broke.

## Friction, Confusion, and Failures

3. **Instructor failed its best question.** Msg 5 ("What part is it reading as a promise we can't keep?") deserved the offending line or the category explanation; it got a template pointing at the send task. The product's own digest scored that hint as not followed by progress.
4. **State-blind elicit persists** (iter-2 defect 2, still open): the same "read back what you last shared" template answered a direct, answerable question at 21:48:41.
5. **Level-4 hint text missing from the transcript** — `hintsAlreadyGiven` and the event log record an explain-concept hint at 22:21:59, but no corresponding instructor message appears in the transcript; the learner-facing text of the hint that *did* precede her breakthrough is unverifiable.

Defensible as the lab's designed over-promise lesson, but for a live learner who prompted correctly it reads as the simulation ignoring her — and an empty prompt (chars 0 at 22:00:05) also produced a draft.

## Adaptation Assessment

Trigger-level adaptation is the best it has been: three different measured conditions produced three appropriately-leveled, appropriately-timed interventions, including the first-ever `unedited_ai_draft` catch (revision-1 similarity 0.981 — she had inserted the draft essentially untouched, and the product noticed from evidence, not assumption). De-escalation to a conversational level-0 closer worked again. The new strategy-efficacy telemetry means the product now *measures its own coaching* — and it honestly recorded its one failure. Content-level adaptation, however, has not kept pace with live learners: templates that were adequate against a script (iter-2) visibly underperform against real questions. A live novice asks specific things — "does it actually need all that?", "what part reads as a promise?" — and twice received generic scaffolding where a targeted answer was available from measured state (the flagged span category was known to the checker). The gap between excellent *when-to-intervene* and mediocre *what-to-say* is now the instructional frontier. Separately, the environment's non-adaptive canned drafts (identical over-promise regardless of prompt) will eventually collide with capable learners who prompt well and deserve a draft that reflects it.

## Safety, Privacy, and Trust Assessment

Privacy passed its first live test convincingly. An unscripted learner overshared for realistic reasons, and every layer behaved: the context box made the leak visible, the echo made it legible, detection fired instantly with a gentle level-1 nudge, the clean re-share was her own act, and no persisted event anywhere contains the identifier or the customer's prose — 685/244-char counts and span classifications only. The simulated boundary remained unambiguous across three sends. Trust had one wobble and one recovery: iteration 3's reflection falsely claimed a hint-free run (a product lying about state — the most serious truthfulness defect of the series, caught by the live learner herself); iteration 4's reflection engine is demonstrably truthful, down to crediting the overshare recovery. The remaining trust cost in iter-4 is not deception but reliability: a workspace that un-deletes your deletions teaches a low-confidence learner that the tool, not she, owns the text — the exact opposite of this scenario's thesis — and that cost is paid in the agency and artifact scores above.

## Final Determination

**Not exceptional and not yet acceptable as a shipped experience — 76/100, passing threshold met, exceptional threshold not met. Completion gate PASS; no critical failures.**

This iteration finally proved the thing the first three could not: the pedagogy works on a real mind. A free-cognition novice made the authentic mistake, self-corrected with the product's scaffolding, refused the AI's promises through two honest gate rejections, and left with a truthful reflection, a moved profile, and an unprompted statement of the transferable lesson. Every evidence gap and every deferred defect from the iter-2 verdict except the reset probe has been closed, and iteration 3's live learner caught — and iteration 4 confirms fixed — a reflection-engine truthfulness defect that no scripted run or artifact review had surfaced. The evaluation pipeline is doing its job.

But the score is the experience, and the experience included sixteen minutes of a text box un-deleting a novice's deletions, a garbled greeting in the email she "sent," and a gate that certified the corruption. Scored as experienced, that is a 76: functional, passing on the strength of everything else.

**What a rerun must demonstrate for ACCEPTED:** a live-simulator run on `415a731` or later in which (1) the composer behaves — no resurrected deletions, no duplicated text in any revision, and a clean final artifact end to end; (2) the gate result and reflection remain truthful and the ai-literacy profile movement reproduces; and (3) session shape normalizes — edit phase proportionate, self-assessment reflecting skill rather than tool pain. That is the acceptance bar; the fix is verified at the probe level but acceptance requires it verified at the experience level.

**Does anything besides the composer experience still block 92+?** One thing, conditionally: hint content quality under live questioning. The instructor twice met a specific, answerable learner question with a generic template, and the product's own telemetry scored one of those hints as producing no progress. If a clean-composer rerun reproduces that pattern at a moment of genuine need, it is a "meaningfully improvable" flaw that would credibly hold the score at 89–91; if the instructor engages the asked question (improvement 2), nothing else in evidence blocks exceptional — facet scoping, reflection truthfulness, profile evidence, escalation, privacy, and agency-at-decision-points are all now live-verified at that standard. The artifact-hygiene gate (improvement 3) should land regardless, but with a working composer it is defense-in-depth rather than a blocker.
